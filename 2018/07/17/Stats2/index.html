Building Blocks of Statistical Inference

CLT (Central Limit Therom)

About sample distribution:

For sufficiently large samples (>=30), the sample mean $\bar{x}$ will be normally distributed

with mean = $\mu$ & standard deviation = $\frac{\sigma}{n}$,

where $\mu$ is the population mean, $\sigma$ the population standard deviation, and n is the sample size.

The CLT can be used to say that the sample means of sufficiently large samples are normally distributed regardless of the distribution of the underlying population; the underlying population could be skewed or symmetric and could be unimodal or bimodal and the results of the CLT will still hold.

Interpretation of 95% confidence value
Even though the confidence interval was derived using a probability statement, the probability really only applies to the sample mean. The sample mean is called a random variable because its value is unknown until the sample is actually selected; however, its behavior can be predicted (that is, it follows a normal distribution) and, as such, the probability it takes on certain values can be derived.
This is not true for the population mean. It is a fixed, but unknown, value and, as such, making probabilistic statements about its value does not make sense. (Note: This a frequentist view of statistics; Bayesian statistics takes the view that parameters are random.) It is also NOT correct to say the sample mean or the sample values fall in the confidence interval with 95% probability or confidence; the confidence applies to the population mean only!
What does ‚Äú95% confidence‚Äù really mean? There is no guarantee that a confidence interval will actually capture the true value of a population parameter. Consider again the example shown in Figure 5. What if the sample mean had been 4.4 instead of 4.6 with a sample standard deviation of 1? The 95% confidence interval would have been (4.1, 4.7). This interval estimate would have missed the true value of the population mean. This happens because 4.4, while a plausible value of the sample mean, is an unlikely value of the sample mean for this sampling distribution. The mathematical notation for the confidence interval is shown below in Figure 6 along with a visual representation of the interval estimate overlaid onto the sampling distribution in our example.
Because a confidence interval is an estimate based on a sample from the population, there is a chance that it could miss the true value of the parameter. This is the reality of sample-based research and inference. What do we do about it? We derive inferential methods that allow us to minimize the chance of such an error. This concept underlies what ‚Äú95% confidence‚Äù in an interval estimate really means.

The confidence level is the proportion of samples in which the calculated confidence interval would be expected to contain the true value of the parameter if the process of randomly selecting a sample from the population and computing the interval estimated were repeated many times.

For example, if we selected 20 random samples from the population and computed a 95% confidence interval for each sample, we would expect that 1 (that is, 5%) confidence interval would miss the true value of the population parameter. This miss will occur because we have a 5% chance of selecting one of the possible-but-unusually small/large sample means from the sampling distribution.

In practice, we will not know the true value of the population parameter, and we will not be able to determine if the confidence interval based on our sample captures the value of the population parameter.

We construct confidence intervals using this approach so that we can minimize, not eliminate, the chance of making such an error. When doing sample-based research, it is not possible to eliminate this kind of error.

Steps of Inference

Once a significant signal is detected with a hypothesis test, the next step is often to report a point and interval estimate to understand that signal.
For example, suppose the hypothesis test found evidence that Œ∏ is bigger than 10. What is your next question? Ok ‚Äì how much bigger than 10? The interval estimate would allow you to address that question. As such, estimation and hypothesis testing are often used in tandem when performing inference; they are two parts of the whole.

Statistical vs. Scientific Significance

Statistical significance and scientific significance are not the same thing.

Increasing the sample size will result in smaller numerical differences but still meeting the statistical significance threshold when performing a hypothesis test.

Because of th is, it is really important to always pair a hypothesis test with a confidence interval so the presence or lack of a signal in the test can be explained in the context of plausible values of the parameter. By examining the corresponding confidence interval, you will be able to determine if signal found in a hypothesis test is scientifically relevant.

statistical significance: Áõ∏ÂØπÁöÑÊòæËëóÊÄß

scientific significance: ÁªùÂØπÁöÑÊòæËëóÊÄß

For example, suppose you were interested in determining whether or not the proportion of overweight adults who reached their weight loss goal after 12 months differed between a group of adults randomly assigned use a mobile health device.

Further suppose that 10,000 subjects were enrolled in t he study and that

  • the p - value of the test was 0.013 (thus, statistically significant at the 0.05 level)
  • but the 95% confidence interval for the difference in the proportion s was (0.02, 0.06).

That is, while the data suggests that the difference is not equal to zero, the data also suggests that the difference between groups is only as large as 6% and could be as small as 2%.

After seeing the results of this study, would you be convinced to buy a mobile health device if you were an overweight adult trying to lose weight?

This is an example of a very large sample size leading to a very small signal being statistically significant, but scientifically negligible.
